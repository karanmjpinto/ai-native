01 · Why now

Intelligence is getting cheap.

When a new technology makes something abundant, its price falls towards zero. It happened to light, to information, to compute. It is happening now to the work of thinking.

01 · Why now

We have watched this before.

As information went abundant online, US newspaper advertising revenue fell by roughly four-fifths in fifteen years. The product was still good. It simply stopped being scarce.

Source: Pew Research Center / News Media Alliance, US newspaper ad revenue, 2005–2020.

01 · Why now

When everyone holds the same model, using it isn’t the edge.

If a prompt can produce the work, the work is no longer scarce. The advantage moves to how you organise everything around the model.

02 · The shift

Most AI today is a chatbot in a tab.

You ask, it answers, you copy the answer back into the real work by hand. Helpful, a little faster, but it never actually touches the business.

02 · The shift

An agent is different. It remembers, and it acts.

An agent carries context between tasks and takes steps on its own — reading, writing, calling tools — instead of waiting for you to ferry every answer across.

02 · The shift

The harness around the model becomes your operating layer.

Everyone rents the same model. What you build around it — the memory, the rules, the tools it can reach — is the part that is actually yours.

03 · The system

Think of it as a stack.

Start at the bottom. The model is the raw engine — and everyone rents the same handful of them.

03 · The system

Add the harness.

The harness is the loop that lets the model plan, use tools and check its own work. It turns a model into something that can run a task end to end.

03 · The system

Add your scaffolding. This layer is yours.

Your memory, your instructions, your context about how the business actually works. The model is rented; this is the one layer nobody else can copy.

03 · The system

Add the utilities.

The tools the system reaches through to touch the real world — your command line, your connectors, your APIs. The hands, not the brain.

03 · The system

Add the agents.

On top sit the agents themselves — the workers that run the stack, one task or a whole workflow at a time.

03 · The system

Five layers. Only one is yours.

Models, harnesses and utilities are bought or rented by everyone. Your scaffolding — the glowing layer — is where the whole advantage lives. The rest of this guide is about getting it right.

04 · The pieces

The layer you own has three parts.

Call it your AI business brain. It is what turns a generic model into something that works the way your business works.

04 · The pieces · Memory

Memory: what your business knows.

Decisions, context, customers, history, the reasons behind past calls. Written down once, it stops living only in people’s heads — and every agent can draw on it.

04 · The pieces · Skills

Skills: how your business does things.

The repeatable procedures — how you quote, onboard, review, ship. Each one written plainly enough that an agent can follow it the same way every time.

04 · The pieces · Software

Bespoke software: the tools only you have.

Small custom tools your agents can call — built cheaply now that they write most of the code. Shaped to your workflow, not bought off a shelf everyone shares.

05 · The moat

Every organisation runs on four levels.

Culture at the top, then expertise, then written procedures, then software at the base. The lower it sits, the more explicit it is — and the cheaper it is to repeat.

05 · The moat

Push work down the levels and you get leverage.

Turn what one expert knows into a written procedure, then into software. Each step makes the knowledge explicit, and the cost of doing it again falls towards nothing.

05 · The moat

Agents cut across every level.

The same agent can absorb your culture, apply expert judgement, follow the procedures and run the software. The clearer each level is written, the more of it an agent can carry.

05 · The moat

Every person you add is another bet that has to land.

A project only works if everyone invests the right effort at the right moment. Multiply those odds together and a team of brilliant, willing people still slows down — not from any single failure, but from the sheer maths of coordinating them all.

Coordination dynamics adapted from Alex Komoroske, “Coordination Headwind: How Organisations Are Like Slime Molds.”

05 · The moat

Agents don’t add the same drag.

A fleet of agents shares one context, holds no competing priorities and never loses the thread between hand-offs. A small team that directs them keeps the odds high where a larger one would have bled them away. That is the real reason to stay lean.

05 · The moat

The moat is the loop, not the tool.

A tool can be bought tomorrow. A loop that turns every job into better memory, faster work and sharper signal compounds — and it does not snap back when a competitor copies your stack.

05 · The moat

When intelligence is cheap, four things stay scarce.

Context about your world, the attention to point it well, the trust of the people you serve, and a brand they remember. Models do not hand you any of these.

06 · Start

Everyone becomes a manager of agents.

Your job shifts from doing the work to directing it — setting context, choosing what matters, judging the output. Less typing, more deciding.

06 · Start

Feed the context loop.

Every note you capture, every decision you record, makes tomorrow’s agents better. The work and the memory are the same act — do it once, reuse it forever.

06 · Start

Start with what you already use.

No platform to buy. A folder of plain notes — markdown, Obsidian, whatever you have — is enough to begin building the memory layer today.

06 · Start

Aim at a North Star. Move in safe steps.

Don’t bet everything on one giant leap. Fix a direction worth years of work, then take the next small step that clearly pays for itself. Each one locks in value and teaches you the ground before you commit to the next.

06 · Start

Don’t hand AI to your old organisation. Build a new one around it.

The motor only paid off when the factory was redrawn around it. Same moment, same move. Start with one process, write it down, and let the loop compound.
